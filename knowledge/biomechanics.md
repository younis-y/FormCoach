## Squat Biomechanics

### Ideal Form
- Feet shoulder-width apart, toes angled 15-30° outward
- Hips break first, then knees track over toes
- Neutral spine maintained throughout — avoid excessive forward lean
- Depth: hip crease at or below the knee (parallel or below)
- Weight distributed mid-foot to heel — not on toes
- Knees track in line with 2nd/3rd toe — no inward collapse (valgus)

### Common Faults & Root Causes

| Fault | Symptom | Root Cause | Corrective |
|---|---|---|---|
| Knee Valgus | Knees collapse inward | Weak gluteus medius, tight adductors | Banded clamshells, lateral band walks |
| Butt Wink | Posterior pelvic tilt at bottom | Tight hip flexors, limited ankle dorsiflexion | 90/90 hip stretch, elevated heel squats |
| Forward Lean | Excessive trunk forward tilt | Weak thoracic extensors, tight hip flexors | Front squats, thoracic extension drills |
| Heel Rise | Heels lift off floor | Limited ankle dorsiflexion (< 35°) | Wall ankle rocks, heel-elevated squats |
| Asymmetric Descent | One hip drops lower | Unilateral hip/ankle mobility deficit | Single-leg RDLs, pistol squat progressions |
| Shallow Depth | Not reaching parallel | Hip flexor tightness, ankle restriction | Goblet squat holds, hip 90/90 stretches |

### Kinetic Chain: Squat
```
Ankle dorsiflexion → Knee tracking → Hip hinge → Lumbar stability → Thoracic extension
```
- Limitation at any point cascades upward
- Example: Restricted ankle → forward lean → excessive lumbar flexion → injury risk

### Joint Angle Benchmarks
- Knee flexion at bottom: 100-130°
- Hip flexion at bottom: 80-110°
- Ankle dorsiflexion: 30-45°
- Trunk angle from vertical: < 45°

## Push-Up Biomechanics

### Ideal Form
- Hands slightly wider than shoulder-width
- Fingers forward or slightly angled out
- Body in a rigid plank — straight line from head to heels
- Elbows at 45° from torso (not flared to 90°)
- Full range: chest 2" from floor to full arm extension
- Scapulae protract at top, retract at bottom

### Common Faults & Root Causes

| Fault | Symptom | Root Cause | Corrective |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hip Sag | Hips drop below body line | Weak core (transverse abdominis) | Dead bugs, plank holds, hollow body |
| Hip Pike | Hips rise above body line | Compensating for weak chest/triceps | Eccentric push-ups, band-assisted |
| Elbow Flare | Elbows > 60° from body | Weak serratus anterior, tight pecs | Wall slides, scap push-ups |
| Shallow Depth | Chest doesn't approach floor | Insufficient strength at bottom ROM | Negative/eccentric push-ups |
| Head Drop | Neck cranes forward | Poor cervical awareness | Chin tuck cue, tennis ball under chin |
| Scapular Winging | Shoulder blades stick out | Weak serratus anterior | Serratus punches, push-up plus |

### Kinetic Chain: Push-Up
```
Wrist alignment → Elbow angle → Shoulder stability → Scapular control → Core rigidity → Hip alignment
```

## Deadlift Biomechanics

### Ideal Form
- Feet hip-width apart, bar over mid-foot
- Hips above knees, shoulders above hips
- Neutral spine — natural lordotic curve maintained
- Bar stays close to body throughout (shins, thighs)
- Drive through heels, extend hips and knees simultaneously
- Lockout: full hip extension, shoulders back, no hyperextension

### Common Faults & Root Causes

| Fault | Symptom | Root Cause | Corrective |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rounded Lower Back | Lumbar flexion under load | Weak erector spinae, poor hip hinge pattern | RDLs, good mornings, hip hinge drills |
| Bar Drift | Bar moves away from body | Weak lats, poor setup | Lat engagement cue ("bend the bar"), rows |
| Knee Cave | Valgus during lift-off | Weak glutes, tight adductors | Banded deadlifts, lateral band walks |
| Hips Rise First | Hips shoot up before bar moves | Quad weakness or poor positioning | Pause deadlifts, front squats |
| Hyperextension | Excessive lean-back at top | Misunderstanding lockout | Glute squeeze cue, stop at vertical |

## Lunge Biomechanics

### Ideal Form
- Step length creates 90° at both knees at bottom
- Front knee tracks over 2nd toe — doesn't pass toes excessively
- Torso upright, slight forward lean acceptable
- Rear knee approaches but doesn't slam the floor
- Weight distributed 70% front / 30% rear

### Common Faults & Root Causes

| Fault | Symptom | Root Cause | Corrective |
|---|---|---|---|
| Knee Valgus | Front knee collapses inward | Weak VMO and glute medius | Single-leg squats to box, band walks |
| Short Step | Knees too far forward | Tight hip flexors | Cue "big step", hip flexor stretches |
| Torso Collapse | Excessive forward lean | Weak core, tight hip flexors | Overhead lunges, pallof press |
| Wobble/Instability | Lateral sway | Poor ankle/hip stability | Single-leg RDL, balance board work |

## Overhead Press Biomechanics

### Ideal Form
- Feet hip-width, slight stagger OK
- Bar starts at clavicle level, elbows slightly in front
- Press in a slight arc around the face
- Full lockout overhead, bar over mid-foot
- Rib cage down — avoid excessive lumbar extension

### Common Faults & Root Causes

| Fault | Symptom | Root Cause | Corrective |
|---|---|---|---|
| Excessive Arch | Lumbar hyperextension | Weak core, tight lats | Brace cue, dead bugs, standing abs |
| Forward Press Path | Bar drifts forward | Tight thoracic spine, poor mobility | Thoracic extensions, wall slides |
| Elbow Flare | Elbows splay outward | Tight external rotators | Band pull-aparts, face pulls |
| Neck Strain | Chin jutting forward | Compensating for mobility deficit | Chin tuck, cervical retraction drills |

## Bilateral Asymmetry

### What It Means
- > 10% difference between left and right = **mild asymmetry** (monitor)
- > 15% difference = **moderate asymmetry** (address with unilateral work)
- > 20% difference = **significant asymmetry** (potential injury risk)

### Common Causes
- Dominant-side compensation
- Previous injury creating protective movement patterns
- Muscle imbalances from sport-specific training
- Structural differences (leg length, hip anatomy)

### Corrective Strategy
1. **Identify the weaker side** via asymmetry data
2. **Start exercises on the weaker side** — match reps on the strong side (don't exceed)
3. **Unilateral exercises**: Bulgarian split squats, single-arm rows, single-leg RDLs
4. **Reassess every 2 weeks** using the bilateral comparison data

## Fatigue & Injury Risk

### How Form Degrades
- First 3-5 reps: technique is maximal
- Rep 6-8: stabiliser muscles fatigue first → compensatory patterns
- Rep 9+: prime movers fatigue → form breakdown accelerates
- Typical degradation pattern: 2-5% per rep after the "fatigue inflection point"

### When to Stop a Set
- Form score drops below 60
- Degradation rate exceeds 5% per rep
- Predicted injury rep is ≤ 2 reps away
- Any "danger" severity alert persists for > 2 consecutive reps

### Recovery Recommendations
- Rest 2-3 minutes between heavy compound sets
- If fatigue prediction triggered: reduce weight by 10% or add a rep in reserve
- Active recovery: light movement between sets (walking, arm circles)

## Rehab Progressions

### Return-to-Exercise Principles
1. **Pain-free ROM first** — can you move through the range without pain?
2. **Bodyweight before load** — master the pattern unloaded
3. **Bilateral before unilateral** — stability before balance challenge
4. **Slow before fast** — controlled tempo (3-1-3) before normal speed
5. **Low volume first** — 2 sets of 5 before 3 sets of 10

### Common Rehab Adjustments
| Injury Area | Exercise Modification | Form Threshold Adjustment |
|---|---|---|
| Knee (ACL/meniscus) | Limit squat depth to 90°, avoid deep knee flexion | Raise min knee angle from 90° to 120° |
| Lower Back (disc) | Reduce forward lean tolerance, avoid loaded flexion | Strict trunk angle < 30° from vertical |
| Shoulder (rotator cuff) | Limit push-up depth, avoid overhead press initially | Reduce elbow extension ROM requirement |
| Ankle (sprain) | Allow heel elevation, reduce dorsiflexion requirement | Lower min dorsiflexion from 35° to 20° |
| Hip (impingement) | Limit squat depth, wider stance | Raise min hip angle, reduce depth requirement |

### Phase Progression (General)
1. **Phase 1 (Week 1-2)**: Pain-free ROM, bodyweight only, 2×5 reps
2. **Phase 2 (Week 3-4)**: Light resistance, 2×8 reps, normal tempo
3. **Phase 3 (Week 5-6)**: Moderate load, 3×8 reps, introduce unilateral
4. **Phase 4 (Week 7-8)**: Full program, normal volume, sport-specific movements

## Movement Snacks

### What Are Movement Snacks?
- Brief (30-90 second) movement breaks taken throughout the day
- Combat the negative effects of prolonged sitting
- Not a workout — just enough to reset posture and activate key muscles

### Example Movement Snacks

| Snack | Duration | Target | Instructions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wall Angels | 30s | Thoracic mobility | Back against wall, slide arms up and down |
| Hip Circles | 30s | Hip mobility | Standing, draw large circles with each knee |
| Calf Raises | 30s | Ankle/calf | Slow raise and lower on edge of step |
| Doorway Chest Stretch | 30s | Pec flexibility | Arms on doorframe, lean through gently |
| Glute Bridges | 45s | Glute activation | Squeeze and hold 3s at top, 8-10 reps |
| Cat-Cow | 30s | Spinal mobility | On all fours, alternate arching and rounding |
| Band Pull-Aparts | 30s | Scapular | Light band, squeeze shoulder blades together |
| Goblet Squat Hold | 45s | Hip/ankle | Hold at bottom of squat, push knees out |
